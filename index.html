Subtheme: Corporate Landlords & Evictions

Dataset

The dataset I worked with highlights corporate ownership and owner occupancy rates in different Boston neighborhoods over a 20 year time period (2004 to 2024). The data comes from property assessment records. Additionally, this dataset shows different demographic data and housing data (including number of vacant units and occupied units). From the metadata corporate ownership is defined by identifying corporations “with markers such as “LLC”...“Corp”, “Realty”..."Bank"...”. There is a note in the metadata that some corporate-owned properties may not be classified as such or other properties may be misclassified. Owner occupancy rates are defined as the rates of properties that are occupied by property owners. The data can be accessed with permission from 11.C85 professors and the Warren Group.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

There were several limitations in the data that prevented me from doing a more thorough analysis, such as not having data that was collected every year (data was the same every year for several measures). Only having corporate ownership rates and owner occupancy rates be responsive to the year made it more difficult to show relationships with other variables over time. Additionally there was data that would have been very helpful to supplement and bolster my analysis such as income data, rental rates, and more elaborate housing data. Another limitation was just my technical skills when it came to doing specific tasks primarily around merging and joining spatial data in Tableau.

However, from my analysis I was able to identify a few patterns or highlights. Neighborhoods like South Boston Waterfront and Downtown have seen the most changes in corporate ownership and have the highest corporate ownership today–this is likely due to new construction and investment in these neighborhoods. To show this, it would be great to have accurate property data that showed total units over time which if there was new construction or speculation. Additionally, these neighborhoods have high White populations which I am curious if is a historic trend. It would be interesting to see data on property values by neighborhood and median income in these neighborhoods over time to understand economic and social changes to the neighborhood. With further analysis, it is likely neighborhoods with a high racial minority population which are being most impacted by speculation and evictions. We see this through the maps of race by neighborhood, the bar chart of owner occupancy and vacancy by neighborhood, and the color-coded table which both highlight Roxbury and Dorchester as potential hotspots. With high levels of vacant units it is likely that the owner occupancy rate is due to private buyers that are buying the properties as an asset, and are not living in the property fulltime, likely displacing families who need housing. Additional analysis may be needed to come to an effective conclusion, however, this data tracks with the readings which provide evidence of higher evictions and speculation across minority neighborhoods in Massachusetts.
